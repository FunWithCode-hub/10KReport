
Hi, what is a 10k-report?

A 10-K report is an annual financial report that publicly traded companies in the U.S. must file with the U.S. Securities and Exchange Commission (SEC). It provides a detailed overview of a company’s financial performance and business operations for the entire fiscal year. 📊

Think of it as the most comprehensive official document about a company’s business and financial health.

Key Sections of a 10-K Report
1. Business Overview
Explains what the company does, including:

Products and services

Markets and customers

Business strategy
Example: A company like Apple Inc. explains its products (iPhone, Mac, services, etc.) and global operations.

2. Risk Factors ⚠️
Lists major risks that could affect the business, such as:

Economic downturns

Competition

Supply chain issues

Regulatory changes

Companies must disclose these risks to investors.

3. Financial Statements 💰
Audited financial data, including:

Income Statement (revenue, expenses, profit)

Balance Sheet (assets, liabilities, equity)

Cash Flow Statement

These are usually audited by accounting firms like Deloitte or PwC.

4. Management Discussion & Analysis (MD&A)
Management explains:

Why financial results changed

Business challenges

Strategic outlook

This section helps investors understand the story behind the numbers.

5. Legal Proceedings
Details ongoing lawsuits or regulatory issues.

6. Corporate Governance
Information about:

Board of directors

Executive compensation

Internal controls

10-K vs Other Reports
Report	Frequency	Purpose
10-K	Once per year	Full detailed annual report
10-Q	Quarterly	Shorter quarterly financial updates
8-K	As needed	Reports major events (CEO change, acquisition, etc.)
Why 10-K Reports Matter
Investors and analysts use them to:

Evaluate company performance

Understand risks

Compare companies

Make investment decisions

For example, analysts studying companies like Tesla, Inc. or Microsoft often rely heavily on the 10-K.

💡 Fun fact:
10-K reports can be 200–400+ pages long because they include very detailed financial and operational disclosures.

Thank you, what time of the year a company submits their 10-k report?

Companies submit their 10-K report once per year, and the exact timing depends on when their fiscal year ends and their company size. The deadline rules are set by the U.S. Securities and Exchange Commission.

Typical Timeline
A company must file the 10-K within 60–90 days after the end of its fiscal year. 

Company Type	Deadline After Fiscal Year End
Large accelerated filers (very large companies)	60 days
Accelerated filers (mid-size companies)	75 days
Non-accelerated filers (smaller companies)	90 days
Example (Most Common Case)
Many companies have a fiscal year ending December 31.

In that case, the 10-K is usually filed around:

Early March → large companies

Mid-March → medium companies

End of March → smaller companies 

So investors typically see a lot of 10-K filings between March 1 and March 31 each year.

Important Detail
Companies can file earlier than the deadline if their audited financials are ready. Some companies file in February, especially large ones.

✅ Quick summary:

Filed once per year

Deadline = 60–90 days after fiscal year end

For most companies → March filings.

So the submission happens once a year, for any given year?

Yes 👍 — a 10-K is filed once per fiscal year.

A company submits one 10-K report for each completed fiscal year to the U.S. Securities and Exchange Commission.

Example
If a company’s fiscal year is Jan 1 – Dec 31, 2025:

The 2025 10-K will summarize the entire 2025 financial year

It will typically be filed between February and March 2026

Key Idea
The report is about the previous year, not the current year.

So the timeline usually looks like this:

Fiscal Year Covered	10-K Filing Time
2024	Filed in early 2025
2025	Filed in early 2026
2026	Filed in early 2027
During the Year
Instead of another 10-K, companies submit quarterly updates called Form 10‑Q.

Typical cycle:

3 × 10-Q reports (quarterly updates)

1 × 10-K report (full annual report)

📊 So every year a public company files:

1 annual 10-K

3 quarterly 10-Q reports

occasional Form 8‑K for major events.

✅ Short answer: Yes — one 10-K submission per fiscal year.

What is a CIK in terms of 10-k submission?

A CIK (Central Index Key) is a unique identification number assigned to a company or individual that files documents with the U.S. Securities and Exchange Commission (SEC). 🆔

It is used to identify the filer in the SEC’s EDGAR database, where documents like the Form 10-K, Form 10-Q, and Form 8-K are stored.

What the CIK Looks Like
A CIK is a 10-digit number.

Example:

Apple Inc. → CIK: 0000320193

Tesla, Inc. → CIK: 0001318605

This number never changes, even if the company:

changes its name

merges

moves headquarters.

Why the CIK Is Important
The CIK helps the SEC and analysts:

🔎 Track filings

Every SEC filing is linked to a CIK.

📂 Search the EDGAR system

Investors often search filings by CIK instead of company name.

🧾 Standardize identification

Many companies may have similar names, but the CIK uniquely identifies the filer.

Where You See the CIK in a 10-K
It appears on the first page (cover page) of the filing, usually near:

company name

IRS employer identification number

state of incorporation.

✅ Simple way to think about it:

Company name = human-readable identity

CIK = SEC database ID

So whenever a company submits a 10-K, it is filed under that company’s CIK in the EDGAR system.
